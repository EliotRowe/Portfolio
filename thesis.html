Autonomous Manure Collection System

Developed a manure collection system for a tractor-based pasture sanitation robot for my graduate research project

Background and Purpose

Manure deposition from livestock grazing poses a significant threat to water supplies. This risk limits the number of animals that can graze in a single pasture. The goal of the Greener Pasture Research Project, funded by USDA NIFA and NSF, is to automate the removal of manure from pastures to increase efficiency of livestock agriculture.

To accomplish this, collaborators from CWRU and Virginia Tech retrofitted a John Deere 1025R subcompact tractor to drive autonomously. My project was to develop a manure collection system for the robot that was electrically powered and mounted directly to the bucket of the front-end loader attachment.

Design

  • Used a rotating cylindrical brush to sweep manure off ground into tractor's bucket
  • Manipulator mounted on the bucket with hinge at top of the frame; angle of the frame controlled by a linear actuator
  • Brush mounted to a subframe attached to the frame on slides; this subframe positioned with another linear actuator
  • Positioned the brush with two active degrees of freedom to allow for more flexibility in positioning the robot relative to the collection target
  • Primary collection strategy started with the brush placed on opposite side of manure pile from bucket; brush then swept backwards towards the bucket while it spun
  • Video shows collection sequence in action, collecting cow-like faux manure on concrete
  • Both frames constructed from extruded aluminum T-slot framing to allow for for easy modifications to the design
  • Mounting positions for the linear actuator designed to acheive the desired range of motion and manufactured using a combination of traditional and CNC techniques
  • All major components made from corrosion resistant materials for outdoor operation

Testing

  • Manipulator's performance was tested across three different ground surfaces: grass, straw and concrete
  • Created mixture of water, potting soil, alfalfa and flour designed to simulate real manure for testing procedures
  • Two different formulae of faux manure used to mimic cow and horse manure
  • For each trial, a simulated manure patty formed with mold and placed in front of the robot; autonomous collection sequence then ran
  • Percentage of manure collected calculated from the initial and final mass of faux manure
  • On average, more than 80% of manure was collected in all trials; on concrete, more than 95% was collected

More details on the design and analysis of results can be found in my thesis: hosted on OhioLINK.
